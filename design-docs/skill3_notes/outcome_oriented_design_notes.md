# Outcome-Oriented Design in the Age of AI

## The New Interaction Paradigm: Intent-Based Outcome Specification
* **Traditional UX**: Users manually dictate what computers should do step-by-step through interactions (e.g., searching flights, then hotels, then activities separately and coordinating them).
* **AI-Driven UX**: Introduces the ability to describe the desired *outcome* rather than the *process*. Users specify what they want to achieve, and the system orchestrates the steps.

## Shift from Designing for the Average to the Individual
* **Traditional Focus**: Perfecting static elements like search filters, results pages, and optimizing components to be usable for the majority of customers.
* **New Paradigm**: Identifying different sets of requirements and boundaries that the system operates within to help individual users achieve their specific goals.
* **Metaphor**: Rather than designing a single rigid path through a forest, designers define the boundaries of where paths can go and what makes a good path. The AI then dynamically creates specific paths based on each user's unique needs.

## The Evolving Role of the Designer
* **From Interface to Possibility**: Designers are transitioning from creating static interfaces and single experiences to becoming "architects of possibilities" and orchestrating adaptive experiences.
* **Strategic Focus**: The role shifts away from step-by-step interaction/interface design to strategically automating these aspects while maintaining a greater focus on user goals and final outcomes.
* **Enduring UX Skills**: Despite the shift, fundamental human-centered skills remain crucial—and perhaps more important than ever:
    * User-centric problem-solving
    * Critical thinking
    * Holistic thinking

## Conclusion
* Outcome-oriented design amplifies the importance of human-centered thinking.
* As AI takes over step-by-step execution details, the designer's role becomes more strategic: ensuring technology effectively serves the outcomes that truly matter to users.
